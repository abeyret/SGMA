San Joaquin Central Valley’s Water Problem

Groundwater overdraft, subsidence & equity under California’s Sustainable Groundwater Management Act

01 · Physical cost · TRE Altamira / DWR

The San Joaquin Central Valley is sinking

Land subsidence is measured by InSAR vertical displacement and USGS benchmarks. Hotspots near Corcoran and El Nido show sustained compaction from long-term overdraft.

  • >30 cm/yr peak rates in parts of the valley (Faunt et al., 2016)
  • 14 km³ subsidence volume, 2006–2022 (Knight & Lee, 2024)
  • 9 m historic subsidence, 1925–1977 (USGS benchmark S661)
  • 6.2 ft additional subsidence, 1988–2016 (Benchmark H1251)

Source: TRE Altamira InSAR (CNRA); USGS/DWR benchmark surveys

02 · Overdraft · DWR Bulletin 118

Critically overdrafted groundwater basins

California designates basins where pumping chronically exceeds recharge. The San Joaquin Valley contains the largest cluster of critically overdrafted basins — triggering mandatory SGMA sustainability plans.

  • Overdraft = extraction > natural + artificial recharge
  • 45 GSPs cover the valley under SGMA
  • 6/8 counties still show groundwater decline post-2014

03 · Mechanism

Subsidence stems from overdraft

When hydraulic head drops, effective stress on aquifer grains increases. Fine-grained layers compact permanently — reducing storage and sinking infrastructure built on a fixed grade.

  • Aqueduct grade disrupted → choke points, ↓ conveyance capacity
  • Well casings appear to protrude as land settles around them

06 · Policy · SGMA 2014

Sustainable Groundwater Management Act

California’s first statewide groundwater law requires local Groundwater Sustainability Agencies to adopt Groundwater Sustainability Plans achieving sustainable yield by 2040 (2042 for critically overdrafted basins).

  • — GSPs by DWR determination status (see chart)
  • DWR evaluates GSPs; state intervention if plans are inadequate
  • Kings County basin probation (2024) — first enforcement under SGMA

08 · Who bears the cost?

Agriculture vs. residents under SGMA

SGMA assigns pumping regulation to local GSAs — often dominated by agricultural interests. Domestic wells are typically shallow and unregistered, failing before deep irrigation wells.

Agriculture

Large pumpers, permanent crops, fallowing flexibility, representation on GSA boards.

Residents & small farms

Household wells, limited capital, CalEnviroScreen burden — harm rises even as plans are approved.
